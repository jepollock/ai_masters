\begin{align*}
	P(H|D) &= \frac{P(H)P(D|H)}{P(D)} \\
	\\
    H &= C1 = \text{ car behind door \#1} \\
    D &= M{=}2 = \text{ door \#2 chosen} \\
    P(C1|M{=}2) &= \frac{P(C1)P(M{=}2|C1)}{P(D)} \\
    \\
    P(M{=}2|C1) &= 3/4\\
    P(M{=}2|C2) &= 0\\
    P(M{=}2|C3) &= 1\\

    P(M{=}2) &= 3/4*1/3 + 0*1/3 + 1 * 1/3  \\
    	&= 7/12 \\
    \\
    P(C1|M{=}2) &= \frac{1/3 * 3/4}{7/12} \\
    &= \frac{3/12}{7/12} \\
    &= \frac{3}{7}
\end{align*}	

\section{Being Dutch Booked}

\begin{align*}
P(prick) &= 0.6 \\
P(!prick) &= 0.6 \\
P(prick or !prick) &= 1\\
\end{align*}

a) This breaks the "sum rule", P(prick) + p(!prick) do not sum to 1. Since there are only two events, and they are disjoint, it also breaks the "addition rule" and the "complement rule".

b) Dutch Book

\begin{align*}
	\textbf{P(x)} &= 0.6 = \frac{1}{D} = \frac{3}{5} \\
	\textbf{D} &= \frac{5}{3} \\
	\textbf{payout} &= 100 \\
	\textbf{payout} &= \textbf{price} * D \\
	\textbf{price} &= \frac{\textbf{payout}}{D} = \frac{100}{\frac{5}{3}} \\
	\textbf{price} &= 60
\end{align*}

This leads to a standard dutch book. However, it does require bettors on both bets to guarantee a win.

\begin{center}
	\begin{tabular}{|c | c | c | c | c | c |}
		\hline
		\textbf{Bet} & \textbf{P(x)} &  \textbf{Payout} & \textbf{D} &  \textbf{Price} \\
		\hline		
		\textbf{pricked}  & 0.6 & 100 & $\frac{5}{3}$ &  60 \\
		\hline		
		\textbf{!pricked} & 0.6 & 100 & $\frac{5}{3}$ & 60 \\
		\hline
		 & 1.2 & 100 &  & 120 \\
		\hline		 
	\end{tabular}
\end{center}

c) A more subtle Dutch book would be one where people believe two events are independent, but are actually dependent.


\begin{align*}
P(A,B) &= P(A)P(B|A)\\
\textbf{vs} \\
P(A,B) &= P(A)P(B) \\
\end{align*}

This linkage may be weak, such that $P(B|A) = P(B) + \epsilon$. For example, the 2008 financial crisis. The assumption that mortgage failures were largely independent allowed them to be tranched and securitized. Realizing that they were not independent, and in fact shared other factors (such as the interest rate changing), allowed other investors to profit by taking bets against the entire market.

\section{Maximum Expected Utility}

\begin{align*}
P(rain)	&= 0.3\\
P(!rain) &= 0.7
\end{align*}

a) The robot should maximize utility. To calculate utility, mutiply the probability and marginalise the action.

\begin{center}
	\begin{tabular}{|c | c | c | c |}
		\hline
		\textbf{Action} & \textbf{rain} &  \textbf{no rain} & \textbf{utility}\\
		\hline
		\textbf{deploy umbrella} & 8 * P(rain) & 3 * P(!rain) & 8*0.3+3*0.7 = 4.5\\
		\textbf{no umbrella} & -5 * P(rain)  & 10 * P(!rain) & -5*0.3+10*0.7 = 5.5 \\
		\hline		 
	\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The utility for not deploying the umbrella is 5.5, while the utility of deploying the umbrella is 4.5. The robot should not deploy the umbrella.

b) The robot should be willing to pay a weighted sum of the difference of the expected values - like insurance where you pay a weighted value of the cost of the event.

\begin{align*}
	\text{let U(A,B)} & \text{ be the utility of action A and event B} \\
	\text{let V(A)} & \text{ be the expected value of performing action A} \\
	U(umbrella, rain) &= 8 \\
	V(umbrella) &= 4.5 \\
	U(!umbrella, !rain) &= 10 \\
	V(!umbrella) &= 5.5 \\
	\text{max price} &= (U(umbrella, rain) - V(umbrella))*P(rain) + (U(!umbrella, !rain) - V(!umbrella) * P(!rain)) \\
	&= (8 - 4.5)*0.3 + (10-5.5)*0.7)\\
	&= 3.8
\end{align*}

The robot should be willing to pay up to 3.8 for the weather report.

c) One of the axioms is Transitivity:

\begin{align*}
	\text{if } L >= M and M >= N \text{ then } L >= N \\
\end{align*}

If this axiom is broken, that means there is no sort order possible. This means the robot will produce different results based on the order the lotteries are evaluated. This could (for example) lead a bot to oscillate between actions e.g. (buy, sell) out of (buy, sell, hold) depending on the order they arrive.

\section{complexity: why bother with BP?}

a) N variables with K possible values.

I keep getting confused with this one. Instead of ${X_i}$, use ${X_1}$ for simplicity.

\begin{align*}
	 P(X_1|OBS) &= \frac{P(OBS|X_1)P(X_1)}{P(OBS)} \\
	 P(X_1) &\propto \sum_{x_2}^{k}\sum_{x_3}^{k}...\sum_{X_n}^{k}P(X_1,X_2,X_3,...,X_n)\\
	  &= O(k^{n-1})\\
	 P(OBS) &= \sum_{x_1}^{k}P(OBS|X_1=i)P(X_1) \\
	 &= O(k)\\
\end{align*}

Therefore, the complexity is dominated by ${P(X_1)}$, and the complexity is ${ O(k^{n-1}}$.

b) maximum in-degree d.

\begin{itemize}
	\item Assume in degree sets min in-degree as well.
	\item Therefore, number of summations is ${n}$.
	\item Each node has n/d values.
\end{itemize}

The following is more about the shape than expressing it generically. Additionally, there may be more things that can be done to limit the connectedness of the graph via max inward degree, but I am going for an upper bound, so assume worst case in all nodes.

\begin{align*}
	P(X_1) &\propto \sum_{x_2}^{k}..\sum_{x_{n/d}}^{k}X_a()\sum_{x_2}^{k}..\sum_{x_{n/d}}^{k}X_b()  ... \textbf{ n more iterations} \\
	&= O(n * k^{n/d})
\end{align*}

c) N=30, K=5, d=3

\begin{itemize}
	\item ${K^{n-1} = 5^{29}}$
	\item ${n*K^{n/d} = 30 * 5^{10} = 6 * 5^{11}}$
\end{itemize}

The factorisation reduced the complexity of the problem by a factor of ${10^18}$. The simplified problem has ~48m computations, which should be tractable.

d) The tree structure reveals unused parameters in each step. This allows the independent sub-expressions to be calculated and re-used as constants by higher expressions.
